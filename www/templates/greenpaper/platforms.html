Platforms

Platforms are boring, reusable components, not attempts to try and rationalise a wide range of desperate processes under a single piece of software. They are available to the wider public sector, and exist to make it easier to make great services for users.

Characteristics

  • simple
  • boring
  • well documented
  • self-serve
  • generally avoid imposing a particular UI on the end service
  • emerge

Types

Not all platforms (under the definition of 'make it easier to make great services for users') will be single, centrally hosted things. There will be a mixed economy of:

  • single, centrally hosted shared services (eg payment)
  • simple to install an instance of a service (eg secure logging < need better example)
  • shared investment in opensource projects (ie government supports OS projects it relies on)

Users of platforms

There are 3 types of user:

  • end users of the service the platform appears in (eg someone paying for something)
  • developers and designers who are building on the platform
  • any backoffice staff administrating use of the platform (eg a service team running payment reports)

In the short term, the second groups of users are the most important. A platform that is difficult for a developer to use is not a platform.

Documentation, example code, code libraries in a range of languages are all critical to attracting developers to help build GaaP and to building quality services quickly.

Who runs them

Different platforms will be run by different parts of government, There should not be an assumption that everything is centrally run.
